## Why People File Mandamus Lawsuits Against USCIS

When an immigration case has been sitting with USCIS for months or years beyond normal processing times and every inquiry, congressional request, and ombudsman complaint has produced nothing, a mandamus lawsuit becomes a practical option. It is a federal court action asking a judge to order USCIS to do its job: adjudicate the application that has been sitting on a shelf.

This is not about winning or losing a case on its merits. A mandamus lawsuit does not ask the court to approve your green card or visa. It asks the court to compel USCIS to make a decision, any decision, within a reasonable timeframe.

## Preparing to File

Before your attorney drafts the complaint, you need to build a record showing you exhausted administrative remedies. This means gathering documentation of every attempt you made to resolve the delay through normal channels:

USCIS online case inquiry submissions and any responses received. Records of calls to the USCIS Contact Center, including dates, reference numbers, and any case-specific information provided. Congressional inquiry correspondence, showing that your representative's office contacted USCIS on your behalf and the agency's response (or non-response). USCIS Ombudsman case assistance requests and any resulting communication. Service request numbers from InfoPass or other in-person appointments.

You also need a clear timeline: when you filed the underlying application, what processing step it is stuck on, how long it has been pending, and what the published processing times are for that form type at the relevant service center or field office. USCIS publishes processing times on its website, and a significant deviation from those published times strengthens the case for unreasonable delay.

## The Complaint

The complaint is filed in federal district court. Your attorney will name multiple defendants, typically including the USCIS Director, the director of the relevant service center or field office, the Secretary of Homeland Security, and the Attorney General. This is standard practice; it ensures the court has jurisdiction over all relevant decision-makers.

The complaint will allege that USCIS has a clear, non-discretionary duty to adjudicate your application, that the delay is unreasonable under the TRAC factors, and that you have exhausted administrative remedies. It will request that the court order USCIS to adjudicate the application within a specified period.

Filing requires paying the federal court filing fee, currently $405. The case is assigned to a judge and docketed like any other civil case.

## What Happens After Filing

The government has 60 days to respond to the complaint. This is where the practical dynamics of mandamus cases diverge from most federal litigation.

In a significant number of cases, USCIS adjudicates the application within weeks of being served with the lawsuit. The agency's attorneys at the Department of Justice contact the relevant USCIS office, the case gets moved to the front of the queue, and a decision is issued. When this happens, the government typically files a motion to dismiss the case as moot, since the relief sought (adjudication) has been provided.

This "rocket docket" effect is one of the main reasons people file mandamus actions. Even if the lawsuit itself never reaches a substantive ruling, the act of filing triggers the adjudication that years of inquiries could not.

## If the Case Does Not Resolve Quickly

Sometimes the government does not adjudicate the case promptly after filing. The Department of Justice may file an answer arguing that the delay is not unreasonable, or that the case involves complex security or background checks that justify additional time.

At this stage, the court may schedule a status conference or set a briefing schedule. Your attorney and the government attorney will exchange positions on whether the delay is unreasonable. The court evaluates the TRAC factors and decides whether to order USCIS to act by a specific date.

If the government claims a legitimate reason for the delay (ongoing background investigation, for example), the court may order USCIS to provide status updates at regular intervals rather than setting a hard adjudication deadline. This still provides accountability and a forcing mechanism.

## Realistic Expectations About Outcomes

The most important thing to understand is that mandamus compels action, not a specific result. USCIS could adjudicate your case and approve it. They could also deny it. The mandamus lawsuit does not influence the substantive outcome; it only breaks the logjam of inaction.

That said, in practice, many cases that have been stuck for years are approved once USCIS finally adjudicates them. The delay was often caused by administrative backlog or a lost file rather than a substantive problem with the application.

Denial rates in post-mandamus adjudications are not publicly tracked, but immigration attorneys who regularly file these cases report that approvals significantly outnumber denials.

## Costs

Mandamus cases are typically less expensive than other federal litigation. The legal question is narrow (unreasonable delay, non-discretionary duty, exhaustion of remedies), discovery is minimal, and many cases resolve before reaching a contested hearing.

Total costs depend on whether the case resolves quickly after filing or requires extended litigation. Most attorneys handle mandamus cases on a flat-fee or capped-fee basis rather than hourly billing, which makes costs more predictable.

The federal court filing fee is $405. Attorney's fees vary by firm and complexity but are generally in the range of a few thousand dollars for straightforward cases.

## No Retaliation Risk

One concern people have is whether suing USCIS will anger the agency and lead to a retaliatory denial. This concern is understandable but unfounded. Federal courts take a very serious view of retaliatory government action, and USCIS officers adjudicating the case are bound by the same legal standards they would apply to any application. There is no documented pattern of retaliatory denials following mandamus filings.

In fact, the existence of the lawsuit creates additional scrutiny: if USCIS denies the case shortly after being sued, the denial will be reviewed more carefully for any indication of improper motivation.

## When Mandamus Is Not the Right Tool

Mandamus is specifically for unreasonable delay in adjudication. It is not appropriate for:

Challenging a denial. If USCIS denied your application, the remedy is an administrative appeal (to the AAO or BIA, depending on the case type) or a petition for review in federal court under different legal theories.

Forcing a specific outcome. The court cannot order USCIS to approve your case.

Cases where the delay is within normal processing times. If USCIS says the average processing time for your form is 18 months and you filed 12 months ago, the delay is not yet unreasonable.
