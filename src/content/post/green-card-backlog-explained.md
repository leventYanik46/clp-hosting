## Why Green Card Processing Takes So Long

The U.S. green card system has a structural supply-and-demand problem. Congress sets annual limits on how many immigrant visas can be issued in each category, and for many categories, demand far exceeds supply. The result is a backlog that forces some applicants to wait years or even decades.

If you have filed or are considering a green card application, understanding how this backlog works will help you set realistic expectations and make better strategic decisions about your case.

## Annual Limits and How They Create the Backlog

Federal law caps employment-based green cards at approximately 140,000 per year and family-based green cards at roughly 480,000 per year. These numbers include the principal applicant and their dependents (spouse and children), so the actual number of workers or family petitioners served is lower than the headline figure.

When more people apply in a given category than there are visas available, a queue forms. Your place in that queue is determined by your priority date: for employment cases, this is typically the date your PERM labor certification was filed or, in some categories, the date your I-140 was filed. For family cases, it is the date your I-130 was received by USCIS.

## Per-Country Caps: The Biggest Factor for Many Applicants

No single country can receive more than 7% of the total green cards issued in a given preference category. This rule applies equally to India (population 1.4 billion) and Iceland (population 380,000). The practical effect is that applicants from high-demand countries face dramatically longer waits.

For Indian nationals in the EB-2 and EB-3 employment categories, the backlog currently stretches to many years. Chinese nationals face similar, though somewhat shorter, delays. Applicants born in most other countries often find their category is "current," meaning no wait beyond normal processing time.

## Reading the Visa Bulletin

The State Department publishes the Visa Bulletin monthly, and it contains two charts per category: Final Action Dates and Dates for Filing.

**Final Action Dates** tell you when a visa number is actually available for issuance. If your priority date is before the date shown in your category and country, a visa is available.

**Dates for Filing** indicate when you can submit your adjustment of status application (I-485) or begin consular processing, even if a final visa number is not yet available. USCIS decides each month which chart to honor for I-485 filings.

The difference matters. Using the Dates for Filing chart lets you file your I-485 earlier, which means you get your work permit (EAD) and travel document (Advance Parole) sooner, even though your green card itself will not be issued until your Final Action Date is current.

## Retrogression: When Dates Move Backward

One of the most frustrating aspects of the system is retrogression. This happens when the State Department realizes it has made too many visa numbers available relative to the annual supply. To correct course, it moves the cutoff dates backward, sometimes by months or years.

If you were eligible to file last month but the date moved backward this month, you may no longer be eligible. For applicants who already filed their I-485, retrogression generally does not affect the pending application, but the final green card cannot be issued until the date becomes current again.

Retrogression is most common at the end of the fiscal year (September/October) and tends to affect Indian and Chinese nationals in employment categories disproportionately.

## What You Can Do About the Wait

There is no way to jump the queue, but there are strategic decisions that can help.

If you are in the EB-3 category, your employer may be willing to file a new PERM for the EB-2 category if you hold an advanced degree. In some periods, EB-2 moves faster, though the two categories have converged for Indian nationals in recent years.

If you qualify for the EB-1 category (extraordinary ability, outstanding researcher, or multinational manager), this category is often current or has a much shorter backlog.

The EB-2 National Interest Waiver (NIW) is self-petitioned and does not require employer sponsorship or PERM, though it shares the same per-country queue as regular EB-2.

Maintaining your nonimmigrant status while waiting is critical. H-1B holders can extend beyond the six-year limit in one-year or three-year increments if they have an approved I-140 and their priority date is not current.

For family cases, check whether the petitioner's naturalization would move you to a faster category. This helps in some situations (F2A to immediate relative) but can hurt in others (F2B to F1).

## The Broader Picture

Legislative proposals to eliminate or raise per-country caps resurface regularly in Congress but have not passed as of this writing. The backlog is a known structural issue, and any significant change would require new legislation.

Until that happens, the practical approach is to file as early as possible to lock in your priority date, maintain valid status, and keep all options open in case a faster category becomes available.
