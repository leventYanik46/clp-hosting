The EB-1A is a green card for people at the top of their field who can document it. There's no employer sponsor and no labor certification required, which makes it one of the fastest employment-based routes. The trade-off is a high standard. You qualify either by a one-time major achievement (think a Nobel-level award) or, far more commonly, by meeting at least three of ten regulatory criteria.

## The ten criteria
USCIS lists ten: lesser awards, membership in associations requiring outstanding achievement, published material about you, judging others' work, original contributions of major significance, scholarly authorship, work displayed at exhibitions, leading or critical roles for distinguished organizations, high salary, and commercial success in the performing arts. You need to satisfy at least three.

## It's not just counting boxes
Meeting three criteria gets you past the first stage. USCIS then steps back and asks whether the total record shows sustained acclaim and that you're among the small percentage at the top. Strong petitions don't just tick three boxes. They show depth in each.

## Who self-petitions successfully
Researchers with strong citation records, founders and executives with measurable impact, and professionals with awards, press, and judging roles tend to do well. The common thread is documentation: independent evidence that others in the field recognize your work.

## What evidence carries weight
Independent recommendation letters, citation metrics, media coverage in real outlets, and proof of your role's significance beat self-description every time. The strongest cases let the third-party evidence make the argument.
