## What a Writ of Mandamus Does

A writ of mandamus is a court order directing a government agency to perform a duty it is legally required to perform. In immigration, it is most commonly used when USCIS, a consulate, or another agency has taken an unreasonably long time to adjudicate an application or petition and the applicant has exhausted other options for getting a response.

The legal basis is the Mandamus Act, 28 U.S.C. § 1361, which gives federal district courts jurisdiction to compel an officer or employee of the United States to perform a duty owed to the plaintiff. The Administrative Procedure Act (APA), 5 U.S.C. § 706, also provides a basis for challenging unreasonable agency delay.

## When Mandamus Applies in Immigration Cases

Mandamus is not a tool for disagreeing with a decision. It only applies when the agency has a clear, non-discretionary duty to act and has failed to do so within a reasonable time. The most common scenarios include:

A pending I-485 adjustment of status application that has been waiting for adjudication well beyond normal processing times, often after an interview has already taken place.

An I-130 or I-140 petition stuck in processing for years without any update or communication from USCIS.

A name check or background check that has been pending for an extended period and is holding up an otherwise approvable application.

A naturalization application that has been pending beyond the 120-day statutory deadline after the interview.

Consular processing delays where a visa application has been stuck in "administrative processing" for months or years without explanation.

## The Legal Standard

Courts generally apply the factors from Telecommunications Research & Action Center (TRAC) v. FCC to evaluate whether agency delay is unreasonable:

The time the agency has taken to make a decision compared to the rule of reason. Whether Congress has provided a timetable for agency action. The effect of the delay on human health and welfare. The effect of expediting the delayed action on other agency activities. The nature and extent of the interests prejudiced by the delay. Whether the agency has acted in bad faith.

No single factor is decisive, and courts weigh all of them together. The longer the delay relative to normal processing times and statutory deadlines, the stronger the case.

## What Mandamus Cannot Do

Mandamus cannot force the agency to approve your application. It can only compel the agency to act, meaning to actually adjudicate the case rather than leaving it in limbo. The agency could adjudicate and deny, which is still a legally valid response to a mandamus order.

Mandamus also generally cannot be used to challenge a discretionary decision. If USCIS denied your petition and you disagree with the denial, the remedy is typically an administrative appeal or a different type of federal court challenge, not mandamus.

## Before Filing: Exhaust Other Options

Courts expect you to have tried other avenues before resorting to mandamus. This typically means:

Contacting USCIS through the online case inquiry system. Making an InfoPass appointment (now handled through the USCIS Contact Center). Having your congressional representative's office make a congressional inquiry on your behalf. Requesting expedited processing if eligible. Submitting a case assistance request through the USCIS Ombudsman's office.

Documenting these efforts is important because the court will want to see that you made reasonable attempts to resolve the delay before filing suit.

## How the Process Works

A mandamus action is filed as a civil lawsuit in federal district court, typically in the district where the plaintiff resides or where the agency office handling the case is located. The defendants are usually the USCIS director, the local field office director, the Secretary of Homeland Security, and the Attorney General.

After filing, the government has 60 days to respond (the standard deadline for the federal government to answer a complaint). In many cases, the act of filing itself prompts the agency to adjudicate the case before the government even files a response. This is sometimes called the "rocket docket" effect of mandamus.

If the government does not resolve the case informally, the litigation proceeds. Discovery is limited in mandamus cases since the issue is usually straightforward: has the agency unreasonably delayed action on a specific application? The court may order the agency to adjudicate the application by a specific date.

## Costs and Considerations

Filing a mandamus lawsuit involves federal court filing fees and attorney's fees. The filing fee for a federal civil case is currently $405. Attorney's fees vary, but mandamus cases are generally less complex than other federal litigation because the legal question is narrow.

The timeline also matters. From filing to resolution, many mandamus cases resolve within a few months, especially if the agency adjudicates the case shortly after the suit is filed. Cases that go to full briefing and decision take longer.

There is minimal downside risk. Filing a mandamus action does not negatively affect your underlying immigration case. The agency cannot deny your application as retaliation for suing, and courts take a dim view of any suggestion of retaliatory denial.
