## How Family-Based Green Cards Work

Every year, the U.S. allocates roughly 480,000 immigrant visas to people with qualifying family relationships to U.S. citizens or lawful permanent residents (LPRs). If someone close to you holds citizenship or a green card, this may be your most direct path to permanent residency.

The system splits into two tracks: immediate relatives and preference categories. Which track you fall into determines whether you wait months or decades.

## Immediate Relatives: No Waiting Line

Immediate relatives of U.S. citizens have no annual cap on visa numbers. This group includes:

- Spouses of U.S. citizens
- Unmarried children under 21 of U.S. citizens
- Parents of U.S. citizens (the petitioning citizen must be at least 21)

Because there is no cap, there is no backlog. Once USCIS approves the I-130 petition, you can file for adjustment of status (I-485) or go through consular processing relatively quickly. Total processing time from filing to green card typically runs 12 to 18 months, though USCIS backlogs can stretch that.

## Preference Categories: The Waiting Game

Everyone else falls into one of four preference categories, each with an annual numerical limit:

**F1: Unmarried adult sons and daughters of U.S. citizens.** "Adult" means 21 or older. Current wait times vary by country of chargeability but often exceed 7 years for most nationalities and can reach 20+ years for applicants from Mexico or the Philippines.

**F2A: Spouses and minor children of LPRs.** This category moves faster than the others, with waits typically in the 2 to 4 year range. F2A is the category that LPR spouses fall into, which is why many couples prioritize the LPR spouse's naturalization so the beneficiary can move to the immediate relative category.

**F2B: Unmarried adult sons and daughters of LPRs.** Waits here commonly exceed 6 to 10 years depending on country.

**F3: Married sons and daughters of U.S. citizens.** This category is one of the slowest, with published priority dates often 15+ years behind the current date for high-demand countries.

**F4: Brothers and sisters of adult U.S. citizens.** The longest waits in the system. Some F4 applicants from the Philippines have priority dates from the early 2000s still waiting.

## How to Read the Visa Bulletin

The State Department publishes the Visa Bulletin each month with two charts: "Final Action Dates" and "Dates for Filing." Your priority date is the date USCIS received your I-130 petition. When the bulletin's date advances past your priority date, you can take the next step (filing I-485 or beginning consular processing).

USCIS announces each month whether it will use the Final Action chart or the Dates for Filing chart for adjustment of status applications. The Dates for Filing chart is typically more generous, meaning you may be able to file your I-485 earlier even if your visa number is not yet current under Final Action.

## Per-Country Caps and Why They Matter

No single country can receive more than 7% of the total family preference visas in a given year. For countries with high demand (India, China, Mexico, the Philippines), this cap creates significant additional delays. An F2B applicant from Mexico, for example, will wait years longer than an applicant from a low-demand country with the same priority date.

## The Filing Process

The U.S. citizen or LPR family member (the petitioner) files Form I-130, Petition for Alien Relative, with USCIS. Along with the form, you submit evidence of the qualifying relationship: marriage certificates, birth certificates, adoption records, or other civil documents. USCIS reviews the petition, and if approved, it moves to the National Visa Center (NVC) for preference categories or directly to the I-485 stage for immediate relatives already in the U.S.

For preference category cases, the NVC holds the approved petition until a visa number becomes available. Once current, the beneficiary files either an I-485 (if in the U.S.) or goes through consular processing at a U.S. embassy abroad.

## Common Issues That Slow Things Down

Several things can add months or years to the timeline. Requests for Evidence (RFEs) from USCIS are common when relationship documentation is incomplete. Aging out is a risk for children who turn 21 while waiting in a preference category, potentially moving them from one category to a slower one. The Child Status Protection Act (CSPA) provides some relief, but the calculation is not straightforward.

Address changes, missed notices, and document expiration can all cause delays. Keep your contact information current with USCIS and the NVC, and respond to any request promptly.

## Planning Ahead

If your family member is an LPR rather than a citizen, consider whether naturalization might reclassify your case into a faster category. If you are in an F2A case and your LPR spouse becomes a citizen, you jump to the immediate relative category with no wait. But if you are in F2B and your LPR parent naturalizes, you move to F1, which may actually have a longer backlog. Check the current bulletin before making assumptions.

Family-based immigration is one of the clearest paths to a green card, but the timelines vary enormously depending on the relationship and the beneficiary's country of birth. Understanding where you fit in the system is the first step toward planning realistically.
