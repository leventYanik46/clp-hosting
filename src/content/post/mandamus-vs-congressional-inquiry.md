## Three Tools for a Stuck Immigration Case

When your immigration case has been sitting at USCIS without a decision for months beyond the expected processing time, you have three main escalation options: a USCIS ombudsman request, a congressional inquiry, and a mandamus lawsuit. Each works differently, costs differently, and has a different success profile.

## The USCIS Ombudsman

The Citizenship and Immigration Services Ombudsman is an office within the Department of Homeland Security that assists individuals and employers with case problems. You can submit a case assistance request through their online portal (DHS Form 7001).

**How it works:** The ombudsman reviews your request and may contact USCIS to ask about the status or request prioritization. The ombudsman cannot order USCIS to do anything, but the inquiry itself can sometimes prompt the agency to look at your file.

**Pros:** Free. No attorney required. You can submit online at any time. It creates a record that you sought help through proper channels.

**Cons:** No enforcement power. Response times are unpredictable, often weeks to months. Results vary widely. In practice, many ombudsman requests produce nothing more than a status update confirming the case is still pending.

**Best for:** Early-stage escalation when the case is moderately overdue but you are not yet ready for more aggressive action.

## Congressional Inquiry

Every member of Congress maintains a constituent services office that handles immigration case inquiries. You contact your U.S. Representative or Senator's office, complete their privacy release form, and they send an inquiry to USCIS on your behalf.

**How it works:** The congressional office contacts the USCIS Congressional Relations Unit. USCIS responds to the congressional office with a status update. The office then relays the information to you.

**Pros:** Free. Carries some institutional weight because agencies pay attention to congressional inquiries. Often produces a specific status update with a reason for the delay. Creates a strong paper trail for any future mandamus action.

**Cons:** Like the ombudsman, the inquiry has no binding authority. USCIS can respond with "still processing" and that is the end of it. Results depend partly on how active and engaged your particular congressional office is with immigration cases.

**Best for:** Cases that are significantly overdue, especially after an ombudsman attempt has not produced results. The congressional inquiry often yields more specific information about why the case is stuck (such as a pending background check or a missing document on USCIS's end).

## Mandamus Lawsuit

A mandamus action is a federal lawsuit filed under 28 U.S.C. Section 1361 asking a judge to order USCIS to adjudicate your case. Unlike the other two options, this is an adversarial legal proceeding.

**How it works:** An attorney drafts and files a complaint in federal district court naming USCIS officials as defendants. The government has 60 days to respond. In many cases, the filing itself prompts USCIS to act on the application before the case proceeds to argument.

**Pros:** The only option with actual enforcement power. Has the highest success rate in producing a decision. The "rocket docket" effect means many cases resolve within weeks of filing, not months.

**Cons:** Costs money: attorney fees plus court filing fees. Requires hiring an attorney experienced in federal litigation. Does not guarantee a favorable decision, only that a decision will be made. Not appropriate if the delay is due to visa unavailability rather than agency inaction.

**Best for:** Cases where the delay is extreme (well beyond published processing times), the applicant has exhausted administrative remedies, and the delay is causing real harm (lost job opportunities, family separation, inability to travel).

## Which Should You Try First?

The typical escalation path is: ombudsman first, then congressional inquiry, then mandamus if the first two fail to produce results. This is not a strict rule. If your case is already far beyond processing times and you are losing opportunities, you can file mandamus without waiting for the other two to play out, though having tried them strengthens your case.

Some people try all three simultaneously. A congressional inquiry and ombudsman request can both be active while a mandamus suit is pending. They do not conflict with each other.

## The Strategic Question

The right choice depends on urgency. If you can afford to wait a few more months and want to minimize cost, start with the ombudsman and congressional inquiry. If you are facing a deadline, losing status, or have been waiting far beyond any reasonable timeframe, mandamus is likely the most effective path.
