## The Marriage-Based Green Card Process

If you are married to a U.S. citizen or lawful permanent resident, you may be eligible to apply for a green card based on that relationship. The process looks different depending on whether your spouse is a citizen or LPR, and whether you are already in the United States.

For spouses of U.S. citizens who are already in the U.S. with lawful status (or certain exceptions), the fastest route is concurrent filing: submitting Form I-130 (petition) and Form I-485 (adjustment of status) at the same time. This avoids the sequential delay of waiting for I-130 approval before filing I-485.

## Concurrent Filing: I-130 and I-485 Together

Concurrent filing is available when the beneficiary is the spouse of a U.S. citizen and is physically present in the U.S. When you file both forms together, USCIS processes them as a package. Along with the I-130 and I-485, you can also file Form I-765 (work permit) and Form I-131 (travel document) simultaneously.

The work permit (EAD) and travel document (Advance Parole) are especially useful because they let you work legally and travel abroad while your green card application is pending. Be aware that leaving the U.S. without Advance Parole before it is approved can result in abandonment of your I-485.

## What to File and What It Costs

The core package includes:

Form I-130 with the filing fee, a copy of the marriage certificate, proof of the petitioner's citizenship (passport, naturalization certificate, or birth certificate), and evidence that the marriage is genuine.

Form I-485 with its filing fee. The I-485 fee includes biometrics. You also need a completed medical exam on Form I-693, passport photos, and copies of your immigration history documents (I-94, visa stamps, previous petitions).

Form I-864, the Affidavit of Support, is required to show the petitioning spouse meets the income threshold (generally 125% of the federal poverty guidelines for the household size). If the petitioner does not meet the threshold, a joint sponsor can co-sign.

## Evidence of a Bona Fide Marriage

USCIS looks closely at whether the marriage is genuine. Strong evidence includes joint bank accounts, a shared lease or mortgage, joint utility bills, joint tax returns, health insurance covering both spouses, and photographs together over time. Birth certificates of children in common, if applicable, are among the strongest evidence.

If you were married recently or do not have extensive joint financial records yet, supplement with other evidence: communication records, travel together, affidavits from friends and family who can attest to the relationship.

## The Interview

After USCIS processes the biometrics and reviews the application, they schedule an interview at a local field office. Both spouses must attend. The officer will ask questions to verify the marriage is real: how you met, your daily routine, details about each other's families, living arrangements, and future plans.

Common questions include: When and where did you meet? Who attended the wedding? What did you do last weekend? What side of the bed does your spouse sleep on? The tone varies by officer, but the goal is consistency between both spouses' answers.

Bring originals of all documents submitted with your application, plus any new evidence of the ongoing relationship (recent photos, new joint accounts, updated tax returns).

## Common RFEs in Marriage Cases

Requests for Evidence are common and not a sign of denial. Typical RFEs ask for:

Additional proof of bona fide marriage, especially if the couple has limited joint financial ties. Missing or expired medical exam results (the I-693 is valid for two years from the date of the civil surgeon's signature). Insufficient evidence of the petitioner's income for the I-864. Missing civil documents like birth or marriage certificates with proper translations.

Respond to RFEs within the deadline (usually 87 days). Late or incomplete responses can result in denial.

## Conditional vs. Permanent Residence

If your marriage was less than two years old at the time your green card is approved, you receive conditional residence, valid for two years. Before the two-year mark, you must file Form I-751 to remove conditions. If you are still married, you file jointly with your spouse. If you have divorced, you can file with a waiver showing the marriage was entered in good faith.

If the marriage was more than two years old at approval, you receive a standard 10-year green card.

## LPR Spouse Cases: A Different Timeline

If your spouse is an LPR rather than a citizen, you fall into the F2A preference category. This means there is a waiting period based on visa availability. Currently, F2A wait times range from roughly 2 to 4 years depending on country of chargeability. You cannot concurrently file I-130 and I-485 unless a visa number is immediately available.

Many couples in this situation choose to have the LPR spouse apply for naturalization. Once the spouse becomes a citizen, the beneficiary moves to the immediate relative category and can proceed without waiting for a visa number.
