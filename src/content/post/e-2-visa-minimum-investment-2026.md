The E-2 visa has no official minimum investment amount, which surprises a lot of applicants. Instead, the law asks whether your investment is 'substantial' relative to the business. In practice that means there's no magic number for 2026. There's a proportionality test you have to satisfy.

## Why there's no fixed minimum
Congress didn't set a dollar figure. The standard is whether the investment is substantial in relation to the total cost of buying or establishing the business. A modest amount can be substantial for a small service business. The same amount would be trivial for a manufacturing operation.

## The proportionality test
Officers look at the ratio of your investment to the cost of the enterprise. The lower the total cost, the higher the percentage you're expected to invest. A small business often requires you to invest nearly all of its cost. Larger businesses can satisfy 'substantial' at a lower percentage.

## It must be enough to run the business
Beyond proportionality, the investment must be enough to make the business operational and not 'marginal', meaning it should do more than just earn you a living. Showing the business can generate jobs or meaningful economic activity helps.

## Practical guidance for 2026
Don't fixate on hitting a number you read online. Build a credible business plan, document that the funds are committed and at risk, and make sure the amount realistically launches and sustains the specific business. The right figure is the one that makes your business viable and proportionate.
