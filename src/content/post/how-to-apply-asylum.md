## Asylum in the U.S.: The Basics

Asylum is a form of protection for people who have been persecuted or fear persecution in their home country based on race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or membership in a particular social group. If you are physically present in the United States, regardless of how you entered, you may be eligible to apply.

The single most important rule to know: you must file your asylum application within one year of your last arrival in the United States. Miss that deadline and your case becomes significantly harder, though limited exceptions exist.

## The One-Year Filing Deadline

USCIS takes this deadline seriously. Your Form I-589 (Application for Asylum and for Withholding of Removal) must be received within one year of your most recent entry into the U.S. The clock starts on the date you last crossed the border, not the date you first entered or the date conditions in your home country changed.

There are narrow exceptions: changed country conditions that affect your eligibility, extraordinary circumstances that prevented timely filing (such as serious illness, a pending related application, or ineffective legal assistance), and certain situations involving unaccompanied minors. If you are filing late, you must explain and document the reason.

## Affirmative vs. Defensive Asylum

There are two tracks for asylum, and which one you are on depends on whether you are in removal proceedings.

**Affirmative asylum** applies when you file proactively with USCIS before the government initiates removal proceedings against you. You submit Form I-589 to the USCIS Chicago Lockbox, attend an interview at a USCIS asylum office, and receive a decision. If denied, the case is typically referred to immigration court, where you can renew your asylum claim in defensive proceedings.

**Defensive asylum** applies when you are already in removal proceedings before an immigration judge. This happens if you were apprehended at the border, placed in proceedings after an affirmative denial, or otherwise served with a Notice to Appear. In defensive asylum, the immigration judge hears your case as a defense against removal.

## Credible Fear and the Asylum Process at the Border

If you arrive at a U.S. port of entry or are apprehended near the border and express a fear of returning to your country, you will be referred for a credible fear interview. This is a screening conducted by an asylum officer to determine whether you have a significant possibility of establishing eligibility for asylum.

If you pass the credible fear interview, you are placed in removal proceedings where you can present a full asylum case before an immigration judge. If you do not pass, you can request review by an immigration judge.

## Preparing Your Asylum Application

The I-589 requires detailed information about your personal history, your family, your travel, and the basis for your claim. The core of the application is your declaration: a written personal statement explaining what happened to you, why you fear return, and who persecuted you or would persecute you.

**Evidence to gather:**

Your personal declaration, detailed and specific. Include dates, names, locations, and describe each incident of persecution or threat. Explain how the harm connects to one of the five protected grounds.

Country condition reports. The State Department's annual human rights reports, reports from Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, and other organizations that document conditions relevant to your claim.

Corroborating documents. Police reports, medical records, photographs of injuries, threatening letters, news articles about events you describe, affidavits from witnesses, and any official documents showing your identity and circumstances.

Evidence of your identity. Passport, national ID, birth certificate, or any other identifying documents.

## The Asylum Interview

Affirmative asylum interviews are conducted at a USCIS asylum office. The officer will ask you to describe the persecution you experienced or fear, ask follow-up questions to assess credibility, and evaluate whether your claim falls within the legal definition of asylum.

Bring an interpreter if you are not comfortable in English. You may also bring an attorney or accredited representative, which is strongly recommended. The officer may ask about inconsistencies between your written declaration and oral testimony, so review your statement carefully before the interview.

## After the Interview

The asylum officer makes a recommendation, and a supervisory officer reviews it. For affirmative cases, you typically do not receive a decision at the interview. Decisions are picked up or mailed later.

If approved, you receive asylum status, which allows you to work, apply for a Social Security number, and eventually apply for a green card (one year after the grant of asylum) and later for citizenship.

If not approved and you are not in valid immigration status, your case is referred to immigration court where you can present your claim before a judge.

## Common Mistakes That Weaken Asylum Cases

Filing late without documenting the exception. Providing a vague or inconsistent declaration. Failing to connect the harm to a protected ground. Not submitting country condition evidence. Missing the interview without requesting a reschedule.
