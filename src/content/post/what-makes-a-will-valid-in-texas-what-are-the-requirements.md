## **Does a Will Need to Be Notarized in Texas?**

**No.** A Will in Texas does **not have to be notarized** to be legally valid.
However, most lawyers include a **self-proving affidavit**, which *does* require notarization. This affidavit speeds up probate by confirming that all signing requirements were met.

## **What Is a Self-Proving Affidavit and Why It Matters**

A self-proving affidavit is a sworn statement signed by the testator and witnesses before a notary public. It verifies that the Will was properly executed and can serve as automatic proof in court.

Without this affidavit, witnesses may need to appear in court to verify the Will‚Äôs authenticity.
Adding one saves time, cost, and stress during the probate process.

## **What Happens If a Will Is Not Valid in Texas?**

If a Will fails to meet Texas legal standards, it cannot be admitted to probate. Instead, the estate will be distributed according to Texas intestacy laws‚Äîa rigid, court-determined formula that often ignores personal wishes.

Without a valid Will, you lose the ability to:

* Appoint an executor to handle your estate
* Name guardians for your minor children
* Establish trusts for your heirs
* Direct how taxes, debts, and expenses are paid
* Control who receives your property
