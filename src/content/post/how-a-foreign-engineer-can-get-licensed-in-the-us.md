## 1. Understanding the US Engineering Licensing System

In the United States, engineering licenses are regulated at the state level. This means every state has its own engineering board and its own licensing requirements. However, the licensing steps are generally similar across the country:



1. Credential Evaluation (Education Review)

2. FE Exam (Fundamentals of Engineering)

3. Gaining Required Work Experience

4. PE Exam (Professional Engineering Exam)

5. Application & State Registration


Foreign engineers must complete these steps regardless of previous experience or licensing abroad. However, many states recognize foreign engineering degrees after a formal evaluation.

## 2. Step One: Credential Evaluation

Before any exam or application, foreign-trained engineers must have their degrees evaluated by an approved evaluation service, such as:

* NCEES Credentials Evaluation

* ECE (Educational Credential Evaluators)

* WES (World Education Services)


This evaluation determines whether your degree is equivalent to an ABET-accredited U.S. engineering program.

**If deficiencies exist:**

Some state boards may require additional coursework. Knowing this early prevents delays, which is why proper planning is crucial.



## 3. Step Two: Taking the FE Exam (Fundamentals of Engineering)

The FE Exam is typically the first major milestone.

It:

* Tests foundational engineering knowledge

* Is computer-based

* Is offered year-round

* Must be passed before applying for the PE License


After passing the FE, you become an Engineer-in-Training (EIT) or Engineering Intern (EI).



## 4. Step Three: Gaining Required Work Experience

To become a PE, most states require 3â€“4 years of engineering experience under a licensed PE.

Foreign experience may count partially but is often limited.

Most state boards prefer U.S.-based supervised experience.


This step is heavily tied to your immigration status, making visa planning essential.



## 5. Step Four: Taking the PE Exam (Professional Engineer Exam)



The PE Exam is the final and most important step. It:

* Requires advanced engineering knowledge

* Is discipline-specific (Civil, Mechanical, Electrical, etc.)

* Can only be taken after meeting the stateâ€™s education + experience requirements.

Once passed, you can apply directly to the state board for full licensure
