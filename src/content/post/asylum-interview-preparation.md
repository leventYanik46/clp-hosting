## What Happens at the Asylum Interview

The affirmative asylum interview is the central event in your case. It is where a USCIS asylum officer evaluates your claim, assesses your credibility, and makes a recommendation on whether to grant asylum. Preparation directly affects the outcome.

The interview typically takes place at a USCIS asylum office. It is not a courtroom proceeding; there is no judge. The setting is closer to a formal meeting. The officer, you, your attorney (if you have one), and an interpreter (if needed) are usually in the room.

## Before the Interview

**Review your I-589 thoroughly.** The officer has your application and will ask questions based on what you wrote. If there are any errors, update them before the interview. If circumstances have changed since you filed, prepare a supplemental declaration.

**Prepare a supplemental evidence packet.** Even if you submitted evidence with your application, bring updated country condition reports, any new corroborating documents, and organized copies of everything you previously submitted. Having original documents available is important.

**Practice telling your story.** Your account needs to be consistent, detailed, and organized chronologically. Practice with your attorney or a trusted person. Focus on the key events: what happened, when, where, who was involved, how it connects to a protected ground, and why you cannot return.

## What the Officer Will Ask

The officer's goal is to determine whether you qualify for asylum and whether your testimony is credible. Expect questions in several areas:

**Identity and background.** Confirm your name, date of birth, nationality, and travel history. The officer may ask about your education, work, family, and political or religious affiliations.

**The basis of your claim.** This is the core of the interview. The officer will ask you to describe the persecution you experienced or fear. Be specific: who did what, when, where, and how. If you were harmed, describe the harm in detail. If you received threats, explain exactly what was said and by whom.

**Nexus to a protected ground.** The officer needs to understand why you were targeted. Was it because of your race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or social group membership? You need to articulate this connection clearly.

**Internal relocation.** The officer may ask whether you could have moved to another part of your country to avoid the harm. If relocation was not reasonable (because the persecutor is the government, or the threat is nationwide, or there was no safe area), explain why.

**Country conditions.** Be prepared to discuss conditions in your home country that support your claim. The officer may ask about the current political situation, treatment of people in your group, and whether the government can or will protect you.

**One-year filing deadline.** If there is any question about whether you filed within one year of your arrival, the officer will ask about this. Be ready to explain your filing timeline and any exceptions that apply.

## Credibility: The Key Factor

Asylum officers are trained to assess credibility. This does not mean they expect a perfect recitation of events. Trauma affects memory, and officers understand that. But they do look for internal consistency (your testimony should not contradict your written declaration), detail (vague claims are harder to credit), and demeanor (though officers are trained not to rely solely on demeanor).

Common credibility problems include: significant discrepancies between the I-589 and the interview testimony, inability to provide basic details about claimed events, testimony that conflicts with known country conditions, and prior inconsistent statements to other agencies.

If you made an error on your I-589 or left something out, address it proactively. It is better to correct the record than to have the officer discover an inconsistency.

## The Interpreter

If you are not fluent in English, you should bring a qualified interpreter. USCIS will provide one if you request in advance, but many applicants prefer to bring their own. The interpreter should be fluent in both languages, neutral (not a close family member involved in the case), and familiar with interpreting in formal settings.

If during the interview you feel the interpreter is not accurately conveying your words, speak up immediately. Interpretation errors can create apparent inconsistencies in your testimony.

## After the Interview

The officer makes a recommendation, which is reviewed by a supervisory asylum officer. You typically do not receive a decision at the interview. For affirmative cases, the decision is usually available for pickup or mailed within a few weeks to months, though timelines vary.
